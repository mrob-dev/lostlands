Chapter X  ·  1453 – today

After
Byzantium.

Ottoman Constantinople, the Russian Third Rome, the Greek diaspora and modern Greece, and the Byzantine afterlife in Orthodoxy.

10 min read

The Roman Empire of the East ceased as a sovereign state on the 29th of May 1453. The cultural-religious-institutional structures that the Empire had substantially developed across eleven hundred and twenty-three years substantially continued, however, in a sequence of successor institutions: the Patriarchate of Constantinople (substantially continuous as a institution from the 451 Council of Chalcedon to the present), the Orthodox Church (across the Greek-speaking, the Slavic-speaking, the Romanian-speaking and subsequent national jurisdictions), the Russian state's inheritance of the imperial-Orthodox synthesis (the "Third Rome" doctrine), the Greek diaspora and the 1832 modern Greek state, and the more diffuse cultural-historical legacy that the subsequent Western Renaissance and the modern Orthodox confessional cultures have substantially carried forward.

Ottoman Constantinople

Mehmed II's reconstruction of the conquered city as the Ottoman capital from 1453 onwards substantially produced one of the most urban-administrative transformations of the medieval period. The post-conquest population of the city was approximately fifty to seventy thousand; by the end of the sixteenth century it had been substantially restored to approximately five hundred thousand, substantially exceeding the late-Byzantine population by a factor of ten. The repopulation programme — substantially involving forced transfers (sürgün) of Greek, Armenian, Jewish and Anatolian Turkish populations to the new capital — substantially reconstructed the cosmopolitan-imperial population that the classical Constantinople had substantially possessed.

The Ottoman institutional treatment of the Patriarchate of Constantinople was substantially distinctive. Mehmed II substantially confirmed Gennadios Scholarios as Patriarch in January 1454 — substantially less than eight months after the conquest — with the constitutional position that the Patriarch was the recognised representative of the Orthodox millet (the Orthodox community within the Ottoman state). The millet system substantially extended religious-judicial autonomy to the Orthodox community — including jurisdiction over civil matters of marriage, inheritance and intra-community disputes — under the Patriarchal hierarchy. The arrangement persisted, substantially modified, until the nineteenth-century Tanzimat reforms and the twentieth-century population exchanges.

The Russian Third Rome

The Russian state's inheritance of the Roman-Orthodox imperial tradition was substantially conducted through the 1472 marriage of Grand Prince Ivan III of Moscow to Zoe (subsequently Sophia) Palaiologa, the niece of the last Roman emperor. The subsequent doctrinal-political articulation of the Russian inheritance was substantially expressed in the 1510 letter of the monk Philotheus of Pskov to the Grand Prince Vasily III:

"The old Rome fell on account of its apostate doctrine; the second Rome, which is Constantinople, fell on account of its union with the Latin heretics; but the new Rome, which is the reigning city, the God-preserved Moscow, shines on the whole world more brightly than the sun. The two Romes have fallen, the third stands firm, and a fourth there shall not be."

The Third Rome doctrine — substantially developed across the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries — substantially provided the ideological framework for the Russian imperial-Orthodox synthesis. The title of "Tsar" (substantially derived from "Caesar") was substantially assumed by Ivan IV in 1547; the Russian Patriarchate was substantially established at Moscow in 1589; the Russian imperial double-headed eagle was substantially adopted from the late-Roman imperial heraldry. The Russian-Orthodox imperial state that the doctrine substantially produced persisted substantially until 1917 and substantially conditioned the subsequent Soviet and post-Soviet Russian political-cultural identity.

The Greek diaspora and the Renaissance

The migration of Greek-speaking scholars and manuscript collections to the Italian peninsula in the fifteenth century — substantially commencing with the Council of Florence delegation of 1438–39, substantially expanded by the post-1453 exodus, substantially institutionalised by the Cardinal Bessarion's library and the subsequent Marciana at Venice — substantially produced the principal mechanism by which the Greek classical and Christian inheritance was substantially transmitted to the Italian Renaissance.

The figures of the transmission substantially included: George Gemistos Plethon (c. 1355–1452), the Platonist philosopher whose 1438 Florence lectures substantially produced the revival of Italian Platonism; Cardinal Bessarion (1403–1472), the Greek bishop who substantially became a Cardinal of the Roman Church and substantially donated his library of approximately a thousand Greek manuscripts to the Republic of Venice; Theodore Gaza (c. 1410–1475), the classical translator; Demetrios Chalkokondyles (1423–1511), the Florence and Padua scholar; Janus Lascaris (1445–1535), the Medici-sponsored scholar and editor. The scholarly cumulative effect of the Greek diaspora on the Italian intellectual culture of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries is substantially impossible to overstate.

Modern Greece

The Greek national independence from the Ottoman state — produced by the Greek War of Independence of 1821–1829, substantially supported by the Anglo-French-Russian naval intervention at Navarino in 1827, and substantially constitutionalised by the London Protocol of 1830 — substantially produced the modern Greek state under the 1832 Treaty of Constantinople. The new Kingdom of Greece — substantially comprising the Peloponnese, the central Greek mainland, and the Cyclades islands — was substantially smaller than the Greek-speaking territories of the Ottoman empire and substantially excluded Constantinople, Smyrna, Crete, the Aegean islands, and Epirus and Macedonia.

The subsequent "Megali Idea" (the "Great Idea") — the Greek nationalist project of territorial expansion to substantially incorporate the Greek-speaking lands of the Ottoman empire — substantially pursued through the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, substantially produced territorial expansion through the Balkan Wars (1912–13) and the First World War, but substantially collapsed in the Greek defeat in the Greco-Turkish War of 1919–22 and the subsequent population exchange of 1923 (substantially eliminating the Greek-Orthodox population from Anatolia and the Turkish-Muslim population from Greek territories). The modern Greek state substantially settled into approximately its present boundaries with the 1947 recovery of the Dodecanese from Italy.

The Byzantine afterlife in Orthodoxy

The principal continuing Byzantine institutional legacy in the twenty-first century is the Eastern Orthodox Church — substantially structured as a communion of fifteen autocephalous and autonomous churches, substantially recognising the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople as "first among equals" but substantially without the centralised authority that the Roman Catholic papacy substantially possesses. The Orthodox liturgical tradition — substantially crystallised in its present form in the post-iconoclast Byzantine church and substantially preserved with remarkable continuity across the subsequent eleven hundred years — substantially constitutes the principal cultural-religious inheritance of the Byzantine tradition that the Orthodox faithful of the twenty-first century substantially still encounter.

The cultural-historical inheritance — the Greek language, the Roman law (substantially through the Justinianic codification and subsequent Russian-Greek transmissions), the corpus of Greek classical-and-Christian literature (substantially preserved through the Byzantine scribal tradition and substantially transmitted to the Latin West in the fifteenth-century diaspora), the Orthodox iconographic tradition, the imperial-political model (substantially adopted by Russia and substantially diffused into European political theology), the Hagia Sophia itself standing in Istanbul as physical evidence of the late-antique imperial achievement — substantially constitutes the fundamental cultural inheritance from the eastern Empire to the subsequent world. Constantinople, as a city, has substantially never substantially been replaced; the subsequent global cities have substantially been substantially functionally distinct rather than substantially substitutional. The walls of Theodosius substantially still stand in Istanbul, substantially fragmentary but substantially recognisable, and substantially the visitor who substantially walks along them substantially encounters substantially the physical evidence of the state that substantially defended itself behind them for substantially a thousand years.
