Foreword

What was Prussia,
really?

Most people, when they hear the word, think of three things: spiked helmets, military parades, and a goose-step. They are not entirely wrong. They are also not at all right.

Prussia was, depending on the century you choose, a Baltic pagan tribe, a crusader state, a Lutheran duchy, the most-feared army in Europe, the engine of German unification, a constitutional state that gave us universal public education and the first modern welfare system, and finally — in its last form — a province of a republic, then a province of a dictatorship, then nothing at all. It was abolished by law. The law was Allied Control Council Law No. 46. The date was 25 February 1947. The first line reads, in the original: "The Prussian State, which from early days has been a bearer of militarism and reaction in Germany, has de facto ceased to exist."

That sentence is a misreading of four hundred and twenty-two years of history. The Lost Lands volume on Prussia is an attempt to unwind it — not to defend Prussia (it does not need defending; it is gone) but to recover it. To explain how a sandy, half-Slavic, forest-and-bog province on the Baltic became, briefly, the most important kingdom in Europe. To name its triumphs and its terrible failures. And then to send you walking through the cities, palaces, battlefields, and small towns where it can still be felt — in Berlin and Potsdam, of course, but also in Wrocław and Gdańsk, in Kaliningrad and Olsztyn, in the windbreaks of the East Prussian plain and the gravel paths of Sanssouci.

You are about to read a long book. You can read it in one evening or across a fortnight. When you finish, you will know what Prussia was. And — if you choose — you will know exactly where to go to stand inside it.

  • I The Pagans by the Amber SeaThe Old Prussians, who were not German, and were not called Prussian by themselves. c. 500 – 1230
  • II A Crusade in the ForestThe Teutonic Knights conquer the Baltic and build a state of stone and rule. 1230 – 1525
  • III The First Protestant CountryAlbert of Hohenzollern turns the Order into a Lutheran duchy on a chessboard square of Europe. 1525 – 1618
  • IV The Great Elector and the SandHow Brandenburg-Prussia survived the Thirty Years' War and learned to outwork its geography. 1618 – 1701
  • V A King in KönigsbergFrederick I crowns himself in the cold, and a kingdom is born — on paper, in 1701. 1701 – 1740
  • VI Frederick the GreatA flute-playing king takes Silesia in eight weeks and almost loses everything getting away with it. 1740 – 1786
  • VII Jena, and the Long ReformNapoleon humiliates Prussia in a single day. The state quietly rebuilds itself from scratch. 1806 – 1815
  • VIII Bismarck's Three WarsDenmark in six months, Austria in seven weeks, France in six. Prussia builds Germany. 1862 – 1871
  • IX The Empire That Prussia BuiltFor forty-seven years the King of Prussia was also Emperor of Germany. The state grew rich, anxious, and brittle. 1871 – 1914
  • X The End of the KingsA war the Hohenzollerns could not win, a revolution they could not contain, and an abdication in a hunting lodge. 1914 – 1918
  • XI A Free State, BrieflyThe Weimar-era Free State of Prussia is, surprisingly, a model democracy — until a coup in 1932 ends it. 1918 – 1932
  • XII Erased by AlliesThe Nazis hollowed Prussia out from within; the Allies dissolved what was left and changed the maps. 1933 – 1947
  • XIII The Flight of Twelve MillionThe largest forced migration in European history empties East Prussia, Silesia, and Pomerania of Germans. 1944 – 1950
  • XIV Where Prussia LivesThe country is gone. The schools, the streets, the welfare state, the philosophy departments — those remain. 1947 – today
