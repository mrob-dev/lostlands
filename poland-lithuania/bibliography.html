Bibliography  ·  The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth

What we read,
how we know.

A short bibliography of the works on which this volume relies, with notes for the curious reader.

General histories

Standard surveys

  • Norman Davies, God's Playground: A History of Poland, two volumes, Oxford University Press, 1981 (revised edition 2005). The standard English-language survey; long, opinionated, and indispensable.
  • Robert I. Frost, The Oxford History of Poland-Lithuania, Volume I: The Making of the Polish-Lithuanian Union, 1385–1569, Oxford University Press, 2015. The most thorough recent treatment of the period before and during the Union of Lublin.
  • Daniel Stone, The Polish-Lithuanian State, 1386–1795, University of Washington Press, 2001. A single-volume scholarly survey of the full chronology.

Government and constitution

The Sejm, the elections, the liberum veto

  • Władysław Konopczyński, Liberum veto: studium porównawczo-historyczne, Kraków, 1918. The classic study; partial English translation available in modern editions.
  • Anna Grześkowiak-Krwawicz, Queen Liberty: The Concept of Freedom in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, Brill, 2012. The most important recent treatment of how the szlachta thought about Golden Liberty.
  • The Constitution of 3 May 1791, available in English translation in the National Library of Poland's Polona digital collection. Read the primary text — it is shorter than most modern constitutions.

Society and religion

The multi-confessional, multi-ethnic Commonwealth

  • Janusz Tazbir, A State without Stakes: Polish Religious Toleration in the Sixteenth and Seventeenth Centuries, Polish Interpress Agency, 1973. The standard treatment of Polish toleration in comparative European perspective.
  • Antony Polonsky, The Jews in Poland and Russia, three volumes, Littman Library of Jewish Civilization, 2010–2012. The most comprehensive English-language history of the Jewish population of the Commonwealth and its successor states.
  • Magda Teter, Jews and Heretics in Catholic Poland: A Beleaguered Church in the Post-Reformation Era, Cambridge University Press, 2006. On the limits of toleration in seventeenth-century practice.

The Deluge and the wars

Military history of the seventeenth century

  • Robert I. Frost, The Northern Wars: War, State and Society in Northeastern Europe, 1558–1721, Routledge, 2000. The standard treatment of the Polish-Swedish, Polish-Russian and Polish-Ottoman wars in a single comparative framework.
  • Radosław Sikora, Husaria pod Wiedniem, Erica, 2012. On the cavalry charge at the relief of Vienna; in Polish but the technical content is substantial.
  • Henryk Sienkiewicz, The Deluge, 1886. A novel rather than a history, but the historical research is substantial and the narrative shape has dominated Polish popular understanding of the period. English translation by W.S. Kuniczak (Hippocrene, 1991) is the recommended one.

The partitions

How and why the Commonwealth was extinguished

  • Jerzy Lukowski, The Partitions of Poland 1772, 1793, 1795, Longman, 1999. The most accessible English-language treatment of the partitions in their diplomatic context.
  • Robert Howard Lord, The Second Partition of Poland: A Study in Diplomatic History, Harvard University Press, 1915. Still the most thorough archival treatment of the 1793 partition.

After 1795

Polish nineteenth- and twentieth-century history

  • Brian Porter-Szűcs, Poland in the Modern World: Beyond Martyrdom, Wiley-Blackwell, 2014. The best recent single-volume treatment of the period after the partitions.
  • Timothy Snyder, The Reconstruction of Nations: Poland, Ukraine, Lithuania, Belarus, 1569–1999, Yale University Press, 2003. On how the multi-ethnic Commonwealth became four mono-ethnic successor nations.

Primary sources

Archives and chronicles

  • Volumina Legum, ten volumes, 1732–1782 (modern edition Kraków 1859). The collected statutes and constitutional enactments of the Sejm from 1347 to 1793. The single most important printed primary source for the Commonwealth.
  • Archiwum Główne Akt Dawnych, Warsaw. The central archive of the Commonwealth's surviving records, including the records of the Crown Chancellery and the records returned to Poland by the Treaty of Riga (1921) and subsequent agreements.
