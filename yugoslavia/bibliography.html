Sources

Bibliography
for Yugoslavia.

The Yugoslav scholarship is substantial in English, French, and the successor-state languages. The contemporary best one-volume narrative is Marie-Janine Calic's. The 1990s wars are extensively documented; the ICTY archives at The Hague are the largest single primary-source collection. The pre-1989 communist period is covered by John Lampe and Ivo Banac.

A reader's reference.

General works

1918–2003

  • Calic, Marie-Janine. A History of Yugoslavia. Trans. Dona Geyer. West Lafayette, IN: Purdue University Press, 2019. The contemporary standard one-volume English-language history.
  • Lampe, John R. Yugoslavia as History: Twice There Was a Country. 2nd ed. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2000.
  • Pavlowitch, Stevan K. Hitler's New Disorder: The Second World War in Yugoslavia. London: Hurst, 2008.
  • Ramet, Sabrina P. The Three Yugoslavias: State-Building and Legitimation, 1918–2005. Bloomington: Indiana University Press, 2006.

Chapters I–III — Kingdom and partisans

1918–1945

  • Banac, Ivo. The National Question in Yugoslavia: Origins, History, Politics. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 1984.
  • Pavlowitch, Stevan K. Tito: Yugoslavia's Great Dictator. London: Hurst, 1992.
  • Tomasevich, Jozo. War and Revolution in Yugoslavia, 1941–1945. 2 vols. Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2001. The standard treatment of the Civil War period.
  • Djilas, Milovan. Wartime. Trans. Michael B. Petrovich. New York: Harcourt Brace Jovanovich, 1977. Partisan memoir from one of Tito's senior associates.

Chapters IV–VI — Tito's federation

1945–1980

  • West, Richard. Tito and the Rise and Fall of Yugoslavia. London: Sinclair-Stevenson, 1994.
  • Rusinow, Dennison. The Yugoslav Experiment 1948–1974. Berkeley: University of California Press, 1977.
  • Djilas, Milovan. The New Class: An Analysis of the Communist System. New York: Praeger, 1957. The classic dissident critique.
  • Allcock, John B. Explaining Yugoslavia. London: Hurst, 2000.
  • Niebyl, Donald. Spomenik Monument Database. London: FUEL, 2018. The principal English-language photographic reference for the Yugoslav-era concrete memorials.

Chapters VII–IX — Dissolution and wars

1980–1999

  • Glenny, Misha. The Fall of Yugoslavia. 3rd ed. London: Penguin, 1996.
  • Silber, Laura, and Allan Little. The Death of Yugoslavia. London: Penguin/BBC Books, 1995.
  • Judah, Tim. The Serbs: History, Myth, and the Destruction of Yugoslavia. 3rd ed. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2009.
  • Donia, Robert J. Radovan Karadžić: Architect of the Bosnian Genocide. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2014.
  • Power, Samantha. A Problem from Hell: America and the Age of Genocide. New York: Basic Books, 2002. Includes the Bosnia and Kosovo chapters in the U.S. policy context.
  • Hartmann, Florence. Paix et châtiment: Les guerres secrètes de la politique et de la justice internationales. Paris: Flammarion, 2007. French-language; an insider account of the ICTY by Carla Del Ponte's spokesperson.

Chapter X — Successor states

Post-2003

  • Bieber, Florian. Post-War Bosnia: Ethnicity, Inequality and Public Sector Governance. Basingstoke: Palgrave Macmillan, 2006.
  • Friedman, Francine. Bosnia and Herzegovina: A Polity on the Brink. London: Routledge, 2004.
  • Vujačić, Veljko. Nationalism, Myth, and the State in Russia and Serbia. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2015.

Primary archives

The documentary record

  • The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY), 1993–2017. All public records at www.icty.org (now archived at the UN International Residual Mechanism).
  • The Tito Archive (Arhiv Jugoslavije) in Belgrade.
  • The Museum of Yugoslavia (Dedinje, Belgrade) holds the principal Tito-era state and personal records.
  • The Sarajevo Survival Museum (Tunel D-B) holds documentary material on the 1992–1996 siege.
