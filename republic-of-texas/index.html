Ten years a republic.

Foreword

What was the
Republic of Texas, really?

The product of the settlement policy of the Mexican government, which had — under the empresario system established by Stephen F. Austin's 1821 contract — invited Anglo-American settlers from the United States into the sparsely populated Mexican department of Coahuila y Tejas, on the express condition that they convert to Roman Catholicism, swear loyalty to Mexico, and obey Mexican law.

Within fifteen years there were thirty thousand Anglo-American settlers in Texas, against perhaps four thousand Tejano (Mexican-Texan) residents. The settlers were Protestant, English-speaking, slaveholding (Mexico had abolished slavery in 1829, but had then exempted Texas), and increasingly disinclined to identify their interests with those of a Mexican federal government that was rapidly centralising under President Antonio López de Santa Anna. The Mexican federalist constitution of 1824 was abrogated in 1835. The Texan settlers — alongside many federalist Mexicans elsewhere in the country — rose in revolt. The Texas Declaration of Independence was signed at Washington-on-the-Brazos on the 2nd of March 1836. Six weeks later, on the 21st of April 1836, Sam Houston's army of approximately 900 Texans defeated Santa Anna's army of approximately 1,300 Mexicans at the Battle of San Jacinto in eighteen minutes of fighting. Santa Anna was captured. The Treaty of Velasco — signed under duress — recognised Texan independence.

The republic that resulted was small (about 70,000 settlers by 1840), poor (chronic budget deficits, repudiated bond issues), militarily insecure (continued Mexican raids, hostile Comanche and Kiowa, slow demobilisation), and politically divided between a Houston faction that wanted annexation to the United States and a Mirabeau Lamar faction that wanted independent expansion to the Pacific. Annexation finally arrived in 1845, after substantial American political controversy over the addition of a large slave state to the Union, and after the British government's diplomatic efforts to preserve Texas as an independent counterweight to American expansion had failed. The Mexican-American War that followed annexation produced the United States' acquisition of California, the rest of the Southwest, and the modern southwestern border.

This volume covers the empresario era, the war of 1836, the republican decade, and the annexation. Then we go to Austin (the third capital — the first two were Washington-on-the-Brazos, then Houston), San Antonio (the Alamo), Goliad (the massacre), San Jacinto (the battlefield), Galveston (the principal port), and the Pecos and Nueces country that the republic claimed but never controlled.
