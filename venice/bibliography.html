Bibliography  ·  Venice

What we read,
how we know.

A short bibliography of the works on which this volume relies, with notes for the curious reader.

General histories

Standard surveys

  • John Julius Norwich, A History of Venice, Allen Lane, 1982. The standard one-volume English narrative — readable, opinionated, and still the best place to start.
  • Frederic C. Lane, Venice: A Maritime Republic, Johns Hopkins University Press, 1973. The classic American scholarly treatment, particularly strong on the commercial and economic structure.
  • David Chambers and Brian Pullan, eds., Venice: A Documentary History 1450–1630, Blackwell, 1992. A commented selection of primary sources, indispensable for the Renaissance and early-modern centuries.

Origins and early-medieval period

The lagoon to 1100

  • John the Deacon, Istoria Veneticorum, c.1000. The earliest narrative history of Venice by a Venetian writer, in Latin. A modern edition was published by Pisa University Press in 1999.
  • Roberto Cessi, Le origini del ducato veneziano, Naples, 1951. The classical Italian work on the early ducal period; still substantially uncontested.
  • Sauro Gelichi and Stefano Gasparri, eds., The Age of Affirmation: Venice, the Adriatic and the Hinterland between the 9th and 10th Centuries, Brepols, 2017. Recent multi-author work covering the archaeology of the lagoon's first settlements.

The Fourth Crusade and Stato da Mar

1204 and its consequences

  • Donald M. Nicol, Byzantium and Venice: A Study in Diplomatic and Cultural Relations, Cambridge University Press, 1988. The standard treatment of the Venetian-Byzantine relationship from the early-medieval period to 1453.
  • Geoffrey of Villehardouin, The Conquest of Constantinople, c.1207. First-hand account of the Fourth Crusade by a Crusader who participated in it. Penguin Classics translation by Margaret Shaw.
  • Nicetas Choniates, Historia, c.1215. The Byzantine view of the sack of Constantinople. Translated by Harry Magoulias as O City of Byzantium, Wayne State University Press, 1984.
  • Benjamin Arbel, Trading Nations: Jews and Venetians in the Early Modern Eastern Mediterranean, Brill, 1995. On the commercial and demographic mechanics of the Stato da Mar.

The constitution

How the republic governed itself

  • Stanley Chojnacki, Women and Men in Renaissance Venice, Johns Hopkins University Press, 2000. Substantially recovers the social texture of the patrician class on whose membership the constitution depended.
  • Robert Finlay, Politics in Renaissance Venice, Rutgers University Press, 1980. On the workings of the Maggior Consiglio, the Council of Ten and the ducal election.
  • Jean Bodin, Six Books of the Commonwealth, 1576. A contemporary external view; Bodin's analysis of the Venetian constitution as a mixed-government model influenced later European constitutional theory.

Ottoman wars and the fall

1453 to 1797

  • Lester J. Libby, Venetian Views of the Ottoman Empire from the Peace of 1503 to the War of Cyprus, Sixteenth Century Journal, 9 (1978). On the Venetian ambassadors' reports — the relazioni — from Istanbul.
  • Molly Greene, A Shared World: Christians and Muslims in the Early Modern Mediterranean, Princeton University Press, 2000. Particularly important for the Cretan war and its aftermath.
  • Paul Strathern, The Spirit of Venice, Pegasus, 2012. Accessible single-volume treatment of the long decline.

Imagery and architecture

The city itself

  • John Ruskin, The Stones of Venice, three volumes, 1851–53. The book that made Venice intelligible to the English-reading world and substantially established the modern conservation aesthetic. Long, opinionated, and indispensable.
  • Deborah Howard, The Architectural History of Venice, Yale University Press, revised edition 2002. The standard modern survey of the city's built fabric.
  • Patricia Fortini Brown, Art and Life in Renaissance Venice, Harry N. Abrams, 1997. For the painting and the social environment in which it was commissioned.

Primary sources

State archive and chronicles

  • Archivio di Stato di Venezia, Campo dei Frari, Venice. The state archive of the republic, with continuous holdings from c.900 to 1797 across roughly 70 km of shelving. Catalogue partially available online.
  • Marin Sanudo, Diarii, 58 volumes, 1496–1533. Day-by-day political journal of a Venetian senator, kept for nearly forty years. The single most detailed contemporary source for the late-Renaissance republic.
